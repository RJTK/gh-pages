High dimensional stochastic optimal control problems are usually intractable, with one exception: linear dynamics and quadratic costs.

Remark: This post is modestly technical, but the idea is incredibly powerful. As well, linear quadratic control is the linear regression of optimal control, so anyone interested in continuous and time-dynamic optimization problems would be well served to start with this case.

Remark: (Comparison to MDPs) stochastic optimal control is analogous to partially observable Markov decision processes. The difference is that in the present case both the action space and the state space are continuous (time can be continuous as well), whereas for MDPs, both these spaces are finite.

Introduction

This post is about a certain type of stochastic optimal control problem. To break down what this is, let’s start with the idea of a control problem, using analogies around driving a car. Most people that have driven are familiar with cruise control – you set a fixed speed, and the car automatically regulates the engine to keep the car moving at about the same speed, despite different road conditions, hills, wind, etc. This is a control problem – you want to control the engine to attain some desired behaviour.

Following this example, an optimal control problem would be to regulate the speed of the car while using a minimum amount of fuel. The amount of fuel consumed is the performance criteria and you want to attain the objective (regulate the speed of the car) while optimizing some aspect of performance. Now, if you also explicitly model the random disturbances faced by the vehicle (wind etc.) this becomes a stochastic optimal control problem – you want to regulate the vehicle’s speed, while optimizing the performance criteria, and in such a way which makes optimal use of any statistical knowledge you have about the random environment the regulator is controlling.

A quite general formulation of a stochastic optimal control can be mathematicized in the following optimization problem:

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \underset{u(t) \sim \mathcal{F}_t^\xi}{\text{minimize}} &\quad \mathbb{E}\sum_{t = 0}^\infty \gamma^t g\bigl(x(t), u(t), \xi(t + 1)\bigr)\\ &\quad u(t) \in \mathcal{U}\bigl(x(t)\bigr)\\ &\quad x(t + 1) = f\bigl(x(t), u(t), \xi(t + 1)\bigr), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

where \(u(t)\) is the control signal, \(x(t)\) is the state of the system, \(\xi(t)\) is a random sequence of disturbances (making the problem stochastic), \(g\) is the performance criteria, \(f\) models the dynamics, and \(\mathcal{U}\) models a set of constraints on the control signal. The notation \(u(t) \sim \mathcal{F}_t^\xi\) means that the control signal \(u(t)\) must be $\mathcal{F}_t^ξ$-measurable, which is a fancy way of saying that \(u(t)\) must depend only on disturbances observed at times \(t\) or earlier (the controller cannot look into the future, hence why I’ve indexed the disturbances at time \(t + 1\) instead of time \(t\)). An example of such a control signal is \(u(t) = h\bigl(x(t), \xi(t)\bigr)\), which generates the control through feedback (from the state) and also depends upon the previous observation of \(\xi(t)\); a non-example is \(u(t) = h\bigl(x(t), \xi(t + 1)\bigr)\) because it depends upon the future.

The particular linear-quadratic problem which at the focus of this post is much simpler than the above – there are no constraints (\(\mathcal{U}\) will not make any further appearance), the dynamics \(f\) are linear and the cost function \(g\) is a quadratic form:

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \underset{u(t) \sim \mathcal{F}_t^\xi}{\text{minimize}} &\quad \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}\sum_{t = 0}^\infty \gamma^t \begin{bmatrix}u(t)\\ x(t)\end{bmatrix}^{\mathsf{T}} \begin{bmatrix}R & C\\ C^{\mathsf{T}} & Q\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}u(t)\\ x(t)\end{bmatrix}\\ &\quad x(t + 1) = A x(t) + B u(t) + \xi(t + 1), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

where \[\frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix}u\\ x\end{bmatrix}^{\mathsf{T}} \begin{bmatrix}R & C\\ C^{\mathsf{T}} & Q\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}u\\ x\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}u^{\mathsf{T}} R u + \frac{1}{2} x^{\mathsf{T}} Q x + x^{\mathsf{T}} C^{\mathsf{T}} u.\] By convention, I will let \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\) and \(u \in \mathbb{R}^m\) (i.e., the state is of dimension \(n\) and the control is of dimension \(m\)). We can generically augment the state or control constant $1$s in order to model linear terms like \(q^{\mathsf{T}} x + p^{\mathsf{T}} u\), but I won’t explicitly keep track of this.
